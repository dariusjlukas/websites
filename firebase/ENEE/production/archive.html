The reading assignment for the second class was the first chapter of the book, which dealt with the ethics gap in engineering education. This is referring to the difference between the prevalence of ethical problems in the engineering profession and the lack of training devoted to it in engineering collegiate education. The book cited statistics that the majority of engineering students expect to confront ethical problems in the workforce, combined with a majority of working engineers reporting that they had experienced ethical dilemmas in their careers. This coupled with the majority of students reporting a virtual absence of ethical training in the engineering program supports the authors claim of “a gap between education and experience” (McGinn, 2). The second part of the chapter had to do with the possible ways of bridging this gap, and the author argued that the prefered method would be to have a dedicated ethics course, as opposed to requiring engineering students to take a traditional ethics course or trying to integrate ethics into engineering classes. The advantages to a dedicated engineering ethics class are that it is more directly relevant to engineering students than a standard philosophy-based ethics course and that it would not have to compete for class time as the material would if it were integrated into an existing class. In lecture, we talked about what was covered in the first chapter of the book, and had a discussion within our groups as to our opinion. We then went on to look at three traditional ways of looking at ethics and their shortcomings of oversimplifying the inherent complexities of real-world problems. We also took time to individually come up with traits that we thought a “good” engineer should have, and compared ours with the class. We then took our first look at a case study: Cody Wilson and his publication of 3d-printable gun designs. The ethical problems of the mass-distribution of untraceable firearms are obvious, but there is still much moral ambiguity about the situation. We looked at the case using the traditional methods we learned, acknowledged their shortcomings, and then compared Wilson to our ideas of a “good” engineer. We finished class by looking at the ABET accreditation requirements for engineering majors and looked at how ethics fit in.

The majority of class was spent reading a brief article which had to do with the ambiguity of the word culture and how many views are inadequate. I was honestly surprised when we used these ideas to critique The Ethical Engineer, since we don’t usually question what is said in the book. I was glad we did this, since one of my major complaints so far has been how we seem to take what McGinn says as scripture, even when myself and others disagree with his argument; it was good to see that McGinn is not infallible. Our group,s article criticized anthropologists, arguably to ad hominem, for taking societal groups out of the picture when describing their culture, with the argument being that anthropologists can not claim to know more about a culture than those in the culture and that they cannot treat culture as a static thing. We were also asked to read the article while replacing “anthropologist” with “engineer” and to apply our analysis to engineers. Now, I see what the point of the exercise was, to show that engineers should take into account the dynamics of cultures and that engineers can be wrong. I would, though, like to add that, unlike anthropology, there are many aspects of engineering which are not subject to person beliefs or opinions, they are simply right or wrong. I am referring to one part in particular which read “...(they) rejected anthropologists and...”. In this context, I could see someone arguing with the results of an anthropologist, since the results are often qualitative in nature. On the other hand, this same excerpt read with “engineers” does not lead to the same conclusion. Whereas in the first example there is a chance that a cultural group could be more correct about their culture than an anthropologist, in the second case whomever is rejecting the findings of engineers is simply wrong; there is no room for argument. For example, if I calculate that monomethylhydrazine decomposes at 491K (Rocket Propulsion Elements, Sutton, Biblarz 2010) and someone argues with me that they think it decomposes at different temperatures in their society, they are wrong. This is a major factor separating engineering from liberal arts disciplines in that engineering deals with objective truths, ones that are true regardless of one’s belief in them, versus subjective truths, truths subject to individual opinion. Again, I understand the point of the exercise, but I just wanted to make the important distinction between opinions and fact.

Class was centered around the Ford Pinto case, which involved Ford knowingly shipping cars with the potential of leaking gas in an accident of as little as 20mph. Our general analysis of the case concluded that Ford had taken actions incompatible with the FEREs. On the contrary, though, I would argue that Ford’s design of the car was fair, but rather their miscalculation was in public relations. The decision to place the fuel tank where they did is one of many safety trade offs made when designing the car, and it did not make the Pinto particularly more dangerous than its competitors. It is estimated that between 27 - 180 people died in the Pinto as a result of fires, and with this the total deaths in the Ford Pinto are not noticeably different from its competitors. The argument could be made that had Ford taken further measures those 27 - 180 people would not have died, but this is not necessarily true, and a common though-process mistake. For example, say a car suffers a sheared bolt on one of its suspension push rods, causing one side of the car to fall onto the ground and slide. Obviously, this is an engineering failure. An inexperienced engineer would say that they should simply use a much thicker bolt. The problem with this is that the bolt will not fail, but something else will fail first, likely causing a more expensive damage than a sheared bolt. This is why it is important to design a system for what will fail first. By the way, this exact event did happen at Terps Racing and I was one of the inexperienced engineers who were quickly corrected by a veteran. In Ford’s case, I’m not sure if the gas tank would be my choice for what should fail first, but at the same time if a stationary Pinto was rear-ended at 70mph and the gas tank caught fire, it is not sound to argue that a stronger gas tank would have saved the driver; better the gas tank fail than the cabin.

In class, we analysed the disaster at the Fukushima nuclear reactor plant, which culminated in the release of radioactive material into the atmosphere, which caused the surrounding area to be uninhabitable. Personally, I found the discussion really interesting because I had heard of Fukushima, but I assumed it was either a simple engineering failure or a fluke incident. I didn’t realize that there were people who had analyzed the plant and found that it would be at risk of a Tsunami-earthquake combination, but had been overruled for political and economic reasons. The Cassandra-effect analysis also helped frame the event as one of many similar events instead of a completely unique case. At the time the disaster happened, I remember thinking that it must have been possible to engineer the plant to withstand a tsunami, but I thought doing so would have been overcautious and too costly. I’m betting this is what the people making the decision about the plant’s safety were thinking as well, and taken as a single case Fukushima could be considered a fluke. But, when looked at as one of many Cassandra cases, it becomes easier to argue that higher levels of safety should become the norm across the board.

We finished up one of the last case studies, one of which involved the the Topf & Sons case and the other involved the US ballistic missile defense system. In particular, the Topf & Sons case made a number of good points because it was, in many ways, far more extreme than the other situations. For the most part, it is safe to assume that the engineers involved knew full well the usage of their technology, especially since one of them stated so in a testimony and another went as far as to design and patent a new crematorium “purely as a facility for extermination”. The engineers made the argument that they simply built “innocent ovens”, and that their ovens did not kill anyone. However, McGinn makes a sound argument for why the presence of the ovens allowed the Nazi SS to increase their throughput, and thus they did lead to more deaths. The engineers also made the argument that their contributions were no different than designing an airplane, which also leads to civilian casualties. McGinn brushes this argument off because most warplanes are not designed to kill civilians, but I some are. This argument is more concerning for me because I am considering working for the DoD after graduation, likely working on projects referred to by the euphemism “defense”. While many of the technologies developed are for defence, quite a few are strictly offensive, and the question is raised if it is ethical for engineers to develop weapons at all. Many people would say this is going too far, but the rules of ethics must be universal, and the Topf & Sons case demonstrates that just because one’s work is for the government does not make it ethically sound. On the other hand, if this country, or any country for that matter, stopped producing weapons many civilian lives within its own borders would be at risk, and that country likely would not be around for long. So, the argument centers around how far is too far. For example, one could argue that the Topf & Sons case would not have been an issue to begin with if Poland, France, etc. had developed better weapons because they would have been able to defend their borders and stopped the Nazi advance. Another example would be the U.S. development of the nuclear bomb. Many in the U.S. justify its development as a deterrent to war and its usage as saving U.S. lives, but the use of that technology led to the deaths of between 129,000 and 226,000 people. Based on that, the argument could be made the the engineers involved in creating the atomic bomb are partly responsible for those deaths. Furthermore, an estimated 241,000 to 900,000 people, many civilian, were killed by “standard” methods of bombing in Japan, so the argument could also be made that the Topf & Sons engineer was correct in that designing crematoriums and warplanes are both unethical. Also, we have already differentiated between civilians and warfighters, and by that argument it would have been more ethical to have increased the loss of U.S. servicemen as far as necessary rather than use various technologies to kill civilians. In summary, I don’t know where the lines of blame should be drawn, as the ethics of war are complicated to say the least. I would argue, though, that this fact does not excuse engineers from acting ethically.
